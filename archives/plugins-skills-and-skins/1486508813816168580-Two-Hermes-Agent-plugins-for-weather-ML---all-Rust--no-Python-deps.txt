    I've been porting the entire Python weather stack to Rust — MetPy, Herbie, cfgrib, WRF-Python, all of it. When you cut out Python/eccodes/Fortran from the loop, weather data processing gets absurdly fast. Built two hermes-agent plugins on top of it.
    
    hermes-weather-plugin gives your agent eyes on the atmosphere. 12 tools covering current obs, forecasts, alerts, METAR, SPC severe outlook, NEXRAD radar, and NWP model imagery. The rendering is pure Rust so you get a full 22-field mesoanalysis suite (CAPE, shear, STP, helicity, etc.) in about 18 seconds, individual renders around 177ms. Also exposes 205 met calc functions and full 40-level atmospheric soundings. You don't need a paid model map subscription anymore, your agent just pulls and renders whatever it needs on demand.
    
    https://github.com/FahrenheitResearch/hermes-weather-plugin
    
    hermes-wxtrain-plugin is for the ML side. Tell Hermes what you want to train and it plans the dataset and picks channels, export format, and loss function based on your architecture (swin, diffusion, classical, graph nets). It fetches GRIB via byte-range subsetting (pulling ~325KB per field instead of 80MB files), decodes natively, computes derived fields, and exports training-ready arrays. Supports HRRR, GFS, NAM, RAP, ECMWF, and ERA5.
    
    https://github.com/FahrenheitResearch/hermes-wxtrain-plugin
    
    The speed actually matters in practice. I tested training a weather model doing real-time fetch/decode/compute during training instead of preprocessing a massive dataset ahead of time, and still kept the GPU reasonably busy. Fast enough to skip the "spend 3 days downloading and preprocessing before you start" phase and just iterate. Both plugins are single static binaries with zero Python or Fortran dependencies.
