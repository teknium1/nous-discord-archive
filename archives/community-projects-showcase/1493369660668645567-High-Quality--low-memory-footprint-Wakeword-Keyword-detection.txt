    Hey everyone,
    
    I've been experimenting with wakeword detection models for a while now, and I'm excited to share an open-source, user-friendly version for the community to try out.
    
    For context: a wakeword detection model is a small, always-listening AI model that runs locally on your device. Its only job is to detect a specific trigger phrase (like "Hey Hermes") from your microphone stream in real time. When it hears the word, it fires an event -- and nothing else. It's lightweight by design, because it needs to run 24/7 without draining your CPU.
    
    This becomes really powerful when paired with a Hermes agent. Instead of manually typing commands or clicking to start a conversation, you can simply speak your trigger word, and Hermes wakes up and starts listening. It turns Hermes from a text-based tool you interact with intentionally into something closer to an always-available voice assistant -- without giving up the privacy, local-first nature, or extensibility that makes Hermes worth using in the first place.

[2026-04-13T21:58:05.352000+00:00] neopabo (id=1493369714280239344)
    A few questions to help shape the pipeline for everyone:
    
    1. What wakeword or phrase do you want to use? (e.g. "Hermes", "Hey Hermes", something custom?) And do you care about having multiple fallback phrases, or is one enough?
    
    2. How many voice recordings are you realistically willing to sit down and record? Think in ranges: under 20, 20-100, 100+. No wrong answer -- this just changes which training path fits you best.
    
    3. What's your mic situation? Laptop built-in, USB mic, headset, or something else? And roughly how far from the mic would you be when triggering it?
    
    4. How noisy is your environment usually? Quiet room, background music, roommates/family around, etc.
    
    5. What language do you speak, and would you describe your accent as regional or non-native? (Wakeword models are more accent-sensitive than people expect.)
    
    6. What would annoy you more: the model triggering when you didn't mean it (false positives), or having to repeat yourself because it missed you (false negatives)? Most people lean one way strongly.
    
    7. Where will this run? Laptop, desktop, or a low-power device like a Raspberry Pi?
    
    8. Do you want always-on listening, or would a push-to-activate fallback be useful too?
    
    9. On privacy: are you comfortable with audio being processed locally but temporarily buffered on disk, or do you need everything to stay strictly in-memory?
    
    10. If you opt into the community dataset, would you want the ability to delete your contributed samples later?
